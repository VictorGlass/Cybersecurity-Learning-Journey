# Security Operations Center (SOC)

## Introduction

A Security Operations Center (SOC) is responsible for monitoring, detecting, analyzing, and responding to cybersecurity events within an organization.

---

## SOC Operations

Activities performed by a Cybersecurity Operations Center:

- Monitoring
- Incident Response
- Malware Analysis
- Threat Hunting
- Cyber Threat Intelligence (CTI)
- Detection Engineering
- Security Architecture
- Vulnerability Management
- Attack Simulation
- Digital Forensics

---

## SOC Roles

Different positions commonly found within a SOC:

### SOC Manager

Coordinates SOC teams, defines high-level objectives, and ensures their execution.

---

### Security Analyst

- Reviews alerts.
- Performs triage.
- Identifies false positives.
- Executes predefined response procedures.

---

### CTI Analyst (Cyber Threat Intelligence)

Produces actionable intelligence to:

- Prioritize tasks.
- Provide indicators for monitoring systems.

---

### Threat Hunting Analyst

Performs proactive threat searches based on hypotheses and investigative processes.

---

### Malware Analyst

Performs malware reverse engineering to:

- Discover capabilities.
- Extract IOCs.
- Understand attacker behavior.

---

### Security Architect

Focuses on security-related issues at infrastructure and technology configuration levels.

---

### Solution Integrator

Deploys and integrates security technologies into customer environments.

---

### Detection Engineer

Develops:

- Detection rules.
- Use cases.
- Monitoring logic based on operational requirements.

---

## SOC Structure

SOC structures vary significantly between organizations.

General considerations:

1. Higher-level coordination is usually performed by the SOC Manager.

2. More mature SOCs tend to include more specialized technical roles.

Examples:
- Tier 3 → Malware Analysts, Architects, Forensics

3. Larger SOCs divide operations into multiple teams.

Examples:
- Tier 2 → Deep alert investigation and response

4. Every organization may implement a different SOC structure.

Examples:
- Tier 1 → Alert triage and false positive analysis

## Ticketing

Ticketing systems are used to manage alerts generated by monitoring solutions.

Benefits:

- Alert prioritization
- Workflow automation
- Response tracking
- Customer communication

A good ticketing system provides:

- Traceability
- Investigation visibility
- Efficient incident management

---

## Security Orchestration, Automation and Response (SOAR)

SOAR connects and coordinates multiple SOC tools.

Capabilities:

- Automates workflows.
- Centralizes security operations.
- Reduces analyst workload.
- Improves response times.
- Increases operational traceability.

---

## Knowledge Base / Documentation

Centralized repository of organizational information.

Typical contents:

- Network infrastructure
- Employee and department information
- Escalation matrix
- Services and technologies
- Critical assets

Objective:

Provide context to improve investigation and response effectiveness.

---

# Key Operational Concepts

## Escalation Matrix

A document that defines escalation paths according to alert severity.

Should include:

- Multiple contacts.
- Backup contacts.
- SLA requirements.

Purpose:

Ensure alerts are escalated efficiently when primary contacts are unavailable.

---

## 24/7 Service

Continuous monitoring typically requires:

- Active personnel 24/7/365
- Rotational shifts
- Multiple SOC locations across regions

---

## KPIs (Key Performance Indicators)

KPIs measure SOC effectiveness and analyst performance.

Purpose:

- Identify bottlenecks.
- Measure operational efficiency.
- Improve team training.

---

## SLAs (Service Level Agreements)

Formal commitments between customers and SOC teams.

Typical SLA objectives:

- Response times.
- Escalation times.
- Incident handling expectations.

Benefits:

- Demonstrates compliance.
- Improves service quality.

---

# Common KPIs

## Basic KPIs

- Total alerts received
- Total alerts handled
- Alerts handled per analyst
- False positives identified
- Total escalations to customers
- Total declared incidents
- Time to Attend (TTA)
- Time to Investigate (TTI)
- Time to Escalate (TTE)
- Time to Close (TTC)
- Time to Resolve (TTR)
- Time to Detect (TTD)
- Time to Mitigate (TTM)

---

## Derived KPIs

### Mean Time to X (MTTX)

Average time required to complete a KPI.

### Median Time to X

More robust measurement against outliers.

---

# SLA Examples

## SLA-001 — Response Time

Time from alert creation to analyst assignment (TTA):

- Critical → 30 minutes
- High → 1 hour
- Medium → 3 hours
- Low → 8 hours

---

## SLA-002 — Escalation Time

Time from alert creation until customer notification (TTE):

- Critical → 1 hour
- High → 2 hours
- Medium → 4 hours
- Low → 10 hours

---

# Final Takeaways

- SOC operations combine people, processes, and technology.
- Monitoring alone is not enough; investigation and response are equally important.
- Documentation and knowledge management improve efficiency.
- KPIs and SLAs are essential for operational maturity.
- Automation and orchestration reduce response times.
